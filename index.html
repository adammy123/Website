ShareVR

A plugin for VR game developers that allows their players to capture and share VR experience on social media

  • When
  • December 2016 - present
  • Northwestern University Entrpreneurship Project

  • Role
  • Software engineer, backend specialist

  • How
  • Unity3d, Amazon Web Services
  • Design Process
  • The product was designed to enable VR users to share their awesome VR experiences to their friends and families. The project aims to spread these experiences to people who have yet to experience VR themselves.
  • How it works
  • VR game developers can integrate our solution into their game, enabling a recording function of the player in a 3rd person perspective. With user-friendly interface, developers can easily adjust their settings to their preference.

  • Recording controls are voice activated using IBM Watson’s API to translate spoken words into text. Through an AWS backend infrastructure, the video file is then automatically uploaded to YouTube. Users can then share these videos with their friends on other social media platforms.
  • Challenges/Learning Outcome
  • Having no prior experience in game engine programming nor backend services, I had to teach myself these skills with only one other member in the team experienced in Unity.

  • Being involved in an entrepreneurial project with people actually using our product both excites and frightens me, but with exceptional teammates, I have learned a lot from them and am ever equipped to face new challenges.

Assists

An automated soccer training system that trains a player’s passing skill

  • When
  • January 2017 - present
  • Northwestern University Design Project

  • Role
  • Programmer, electronics engineer, prototyper

  • How
  • Arduino, lots of sensors, lots of soccer balls
  • Design Process
  • The aim was to design and build an automated passing system for Northwestern University varsity soccer players to improve their passing skills. The system had to be portable and be used on artificial turf. The team iterated through several target and system designs and tested them with our intended end users.
  • How it works
  • A player sets up an arrangement of 4-7 targets and a feeder that feeds a ball to the player every 3-4 seconds. Powered by arduino-loaded micro controller chips, a target will light up to indicate the passing target for the current ball.

  • After feeding about 10 balls, the session ends and the player receives performance data - pass accuracy and average touch time - which can be analyzed and compared with teammates.
  • Challenges/Learning Outcome
  • We found it challenging to improve target stability on artificial turf without adding too much weight. There was an overemphasis on keeping the system light to ensure its portability, thus creating several iterations to solve the problem. However, after a later user testing, we learnt that users did not mind the extra weight if the product was easy to transport.

  • In hindsight, we should have conducted more early user testing and interviews to ensure focus on more important aspects.

Trashifai

Smart waste bin that sorts recyclables from trash

  • When
  • Northwestern University - November 2016
  • Wildhacks 2016 (48 hour hackathon)

  • Role
  • Programmer, electronics engineer, prototyper

  • How
  • Arduino, openCV, Clarifai, Twilio

  • Award
  • JP Morgan Chase & Co - Best Social Good Hack
  • Design Process
  • Our goal was to design a sustainable solution using Clarifai’s API, a machine learning image recognition API. We were introduced to this new technology at the opening ceremony of the event and were intrigued by its countless number of applications.
  • How it works
  • When an object is placed on the bin, an ultrasound sensor detects it and signals the Arduino to fire a python script that uses openCV to take a picture of the object. The image is then sent to Clarifai’s API that returns a “trash” or “recyclable” tag, which tells the Arduino which way to turn the motor.

  • Two additional ultrasound sensors are used to detect a nearly full bin, which then calls the python script to send a text message to relevant authorities using Twilio’s API.
  • Challenges/Learning Outcome
  • We spent a considerable amount of time learning how to use the two new APIs introduced to us. As they were relatively new technology, the documentation and support were not as robust as that of older technology. However, this was overcome by consulting the company's on-site personnel and trial and error.

Sumobot

Autonomous robot car that competes in robot-sumo contests

  • When
  • Nanyang Technological University - March 2016

  • Role
  • Programmer, electronics engineer, prototyper

  • How
  • Arduino, lots of sensors

  • Award
  • Inspector Gadget - Most creative use of peripherals
  • Design Process
  • Being one of two coders in the team of five, I designed an optimized algorithm for our robot to win. However, we then realized that hardware, rather than software, would make a bigger difference in the performance of our robot.
  • How it works
  • The robot as two line detectors on its bottom to determine if it is near the edge of the arena, and two pairs of infrared emitters and receivers at the front to determine the location of its enemy.

  • The main offense was its special white cover that was crafted to trick our enemies into thinking that it was at the edge of the arena. This makes our enemy reverse while we push forward.
  • Challenges/Learning Outcome
  • The main challenge we faced was debugging the unreliable sensors that were distributed by the competition organizers. Oftentimes, our algorithm did not seem to work and we did not know if there was a problem with the code, the soldering or simply the hardware itself (even after testing the individual components beforehand).

  • As such, we learnt the need to come up with ways to test specific components in controlled conditions when they are still connected together as a whole.
